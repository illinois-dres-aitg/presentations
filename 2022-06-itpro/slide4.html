The Importance of Semantic Structure

Structural Elements

Page Title

Included because heading semantics (hierarchical labels describing sections of content) should align with the title of the page (especially the top-level headings).

Headings

As pointed out in WCAG 2.1 (Success Criteria 2.4.10 - Section Headings), not all pages require headings. However, any page with content broken down into sections (covers many pages) should have HTML headings.

Landmarks

Semantic regions such as header, nav, main, footer, aside, and search.

As pointed out on the WebAIM page linked below, every page should have one 'main' element, and most pages should have only one 'header' and one 'footer'. All three of these should be top-level elements (direct children of the 'body' element).

WebAIM also warns against the overuse of nav elements (or the navigation role). Generally a page should have a primary navigation region, and perhaps one or two secondary nav regions. Links in the 'footer' do not necessarily need to be enclosed in a nav element.

More than one region of the same type need unique labels so that users can tell them apart. (See this frequently with the 'navigation' role.)

Benefits of Clear Semantic Structure

  • All users benefit from being able to scan the headings on a page and treat them as if they were an outline or table of contents.
  • Screen reader users rely heavily on navigating among pages based on the page title, and within pages among headings and landmark regions.
